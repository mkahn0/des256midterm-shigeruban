About Shigeru Ban

Known for sustainable architectural design and humanitarian work, Shigeru Ban is a Japanese architect who has transformed disaster relief solutions worldwide. Ban studied architecture at the Southern California Institute of Architecture and The Cooper Union, and he founded his private practice in 1985. Ban is famous for incorporating recycled paper as an unconventional and low-cost building material to create strong, lightweight buildings that can be quickly assembled. After the Great Hanshin Earthquake in 1995, Ban founded the Voluntary Architects' Network (VAN), a nonprofit and nongovernmental organization that contributes to disaster relief efforts by providing temporary shelters for victims. Ban’s work has earned him several awards, including the Pritzker Architecture Prize (2014), the Mother Teresa Social Justice Award (2017), the Princess of Asturias Award for Concord (2022), the Praemium Imperiale for Architecture (2024), and the AIA Gold Medal (2026).

Design Innovation

Ban chose to work primarily with paper tubes because of its abundance, versatility, and affordability. Being an inexpensive material that is available globally, paper tubes can be easily and quickly manufactured in large quantities and are able to be waterproofed and fireproofed. Many of his paper tube constructions are temporary, with the intention that the paper tubes be recycled or reused after building deconstruction.

Notable Projects

Paper Log House

1995 - Kobe, Japan

In response to the Great Hanshin Earthquake, the Paper Log House provided temporary housing for displaced residents. It incorporated the use of cardboard paper tubes, beer crates, sandbags, and tent membranes, and they provided shelter and insulation in a visually satisfying structure.

Paper Partition System

The Paper Partition System (PPS) uses paper tubes and fabric panels to create temporary partitions that provide privacy and improved living conditions in emergency shelters. It has been implemented in response to cases of natural disaster, disease, and refugee crises. Below are some examples of the PPS in use.

2004 - Japan

Implemented after the Niigata earthquake in 2004, this was the first use of the PPS. It was intended to provide privacy in the gymnasium shelter, but was instead used as a play area for children and a clinic for the elderly.

2020 - Japan

The PPS was installed to prevent the spread of Coronavirus via droplets during the pandemic.

2022 - Poland, Ukraine, Slovakia, Germany, & France

PPS’s were installed in refugee shelters to support individuals displaced by the Russian invasion of Ukraine. This series of installations marked the first of four phases of the Ukraine Refugee Assistance project.

2025 - Los Angeles, USA

At the Westwood Recreation Center in Santa Monica, the PPS was used in the medical bay to provide privacy for medical staff and patients. This is the most recent use case of the PPS.

Cardboard Cathedral

2013 - Christchurch, New Zealand

After an earthquake damaged the Christchurch Cathedral, a major landmark of the city, the Cardboard Cathedral was constructed using paper tubes to serve as a temporary cathedral and community gathering space.

Social Impact

Shigeru Ban demonstrates that intentionally designed architecture can positively address humanitarian challenges. Using inexpensive paper tubes, the built structures are easy to assemble, disassemble, and transport to areas in crisis. Through projects developed with VAN, the structures help provide shelter, privacy, and community spaces for individuals displaced by natural disasters, public health emergencies, or other disruptive events. Throughout his work, Ban also demonstrates a clear dedication to environmentally friendly architecture with his signature use of the paper tube, a low-cost material with a multitude of uses, and other reusable or recyclable materials.
